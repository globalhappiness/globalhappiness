PROJECT OVERVIEW

 

The World Happiness Report is an annual report that seeks to assess that state of global happiness. First published in 2012, the report is published by a team of independent experts in the fields of economics, psychology, survey analysis, national statistics, health, public policy and more. The report utilizes data collected from Gallup and utilizes different country statistics- GDP per capita, family size, government trust, life expectancy, freedom, generosity, and dystopia residual- to identify patterns and associations between countries the happiness index (Cantrill ladder) and their respective statistics. In using this data, as well as supplementary data from a number of different sources, we seek to provide detailed analysis of the different factors that contribute to happiness.

DATA SOURCES & TECHNICAL PROCESS

 

The primary data source is the World Happiness Report.This data includes years 2015 and 2016. The report offers us several qualitative and quantitative variables. In addition to the World Happiness Report Data, we gathered data from the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs and the CIA World Factbook. We merged the sets together to allow us to do further analysis on the factors that influence happiness. The variables include:

 

Country (ID) - The name of the country. Used to identify happiness levels on an individual and regional scope.

Region - The region of the world and continent each country is located. We used this to create a bubble plot to help identify the happiest regions in the world.

Happiness Score - The score calculated from answers to the World Gallup poll, where respondents were asked to think of a ladder with the best possible life for them being a 10 and the worst possible life being a 0 and to rate their own current lives on that scale. The score is the average ranking each respondent gave on the scale of 0-10.

Happiness Rank - A numerical rank of the countries based on their Happiness Score with a range of 1-158.

Dystopia Residual - A hypothetical number calculated for each of the six categories below, whose value is derived using the lowest national averages in each of those categories.

 

The following variables estimate the extent to which each of the factors affect each countrys happiness score.

-       Economy (GDP per capita) , Family, Life Expectancy, Freedom, Government Trust, Generosity

Questions

Our group had many questions about peoples happiness and the factors that influence it. The first is where are people the happiest? Next we wanted to look at the influence government has on happiness, in other words does government mistrust influence the happiness of the people? After that we wanted to know whether or not freedom and happiness are related. Lastly we wanted to understand how quality of life factors, such as education and life expectancy influence happiness. Before we began to represent the data visually, we ran a regression to understand the relationships between the variables and happiness.

 

Where are people the happiest?

 

We created a choropleth that distinguishes each countrys happiness by different shades of blue. We found multiple geographical trends, for instance it was clear that Norwegian countries are generally happier than countries in Sub-Saharan Africa.

 

Does government corruption affect happiness?

 

To understand the relationship between trust and happiness we coded the data to show happiness with the size of bubbles and trust as the shade. From this visualization, you can see that smaller bubbles (less happy) have a lighter shade (distrust government). Upon further analysis, we decided that this would best be encoded as a scatterplot that compared happiness and government corruption on the axis. By doing this the relationship is more visible, and quantifiable, also it allows the user to utilize a slicer to further drill down using region, country, or happiness rank.

 

Does freedom make you happier?

 

To understand the affect of freedom on happiness score, we created a visualization we plotted each regions happiness score with their freedom score, for two years. This shows the relationship that exists between freedom and happiness. It also shows how changes in freedom are changes in happiness happen together (shown by changes from 2015 to 2016). We ultimately decided that this variable too is better represented using a scatterplot, where a slicer can be utilized.

 

How does quality of life affect happiness?

We decided to look at life expectancy and education level as indicators of the quality of life. We coded this in a scatter plot to visualize the correlation between education and life expectancy and the happiness as the size of the circle. Rather than look at all countries, we wanted to distinguish the quality of life between the happiest 10% and the least happy 10%. In our final model we encoded this variable as a scatterplot that directly compares life expectancy and happiness. This more clearly shows how life expectancy is related to overall happiness.

 

The BIG Question: What variables contribute most to happiness?

There are numerous variables that correlate with happiness, however in order to best represent the data, we want to put forth a dashboard drills down the most important variables, and allows the user to compare this variables between different regions, happiness rank, and across countries.

Design Evolution and Implementation

Our data enables a myriad of potential visualizations. In order to gain a holistic understanding of our data, well begin our visualization with basic statistical graphs- scatter plots for correlation, histograms for distribution, line graphs for time related data.

 

Naturally, our data lends itself to be represented through maps. We plan to use choropleths to easily convey differences between variables. After using maps to gain an understanding of variables impacts on a high level, well delve deeper and examine different variables from a closer perspective. Well examine the data points that seem to demonstrate significant influence on the target. Well inspect patterns and group similar data. An example of a deeper visualization is examining the different attributes of the countries with the highest and lowest Cantrill ladders, GDPs, etc.

 

We found that time series analysis was not adequately captured in a two year period. As a result, we decided to focus less on time and more on other factors such as education, health, and life expectancy as we moved forward.

The dashboard above is what we used in our first draft submission. We decided to use the bubble plot on the top left to list each region and show their happiness scale using color. Instead of just showing happiness by region, we were also interested in learning about how government trust affected these numbers. As illustrated, Australia and New Zealand contain the highest level of trust, which results in them also having the highest level of happiness, on average. We knew we wanted to create a map all along and that it would be easy to implement with our data set, so the plot on the top right of the dashboard was a result of that interest. We decided to make a choropleth map in order to remain consistent with our first graphic, and the scale of colors is listed at the top of the graphic along with the attributed score. Even with a brief glance at the map, its easy to identify that North and South America, Northern Europe, and Australia all have darker shades of blue associated with their countries, and therefore have a higher happiness score. We see the lowest scores in Africa, Asia, and the Middle East.

 

After receiving constructive feedback on our first draft, we decided to make our dashboard more interactive, as to allow the user to drill down on the information being displayed. To do this we created filters at the top of our dashboard that can sort the data by country, region, and happiness rank. We also wanted to put forward the factors that we determined to correlate most highly with happiness. Economy, trust of the government, health/life expectancy, and unemployment were the most important, so we decided to make them an interactive part of our dashboard. The dashboard is encoded so that by either clicking on a country on the choropleth, or by using one of the filters, the country/countries that are selected will stand out on the scatterplots of the important variables (Economy, trust of government etc.). This allows the used to see where that country ranks on each of the important variables.

 

There are a number of choices that we had to make when creating this visualization. Immediately when you look at the dashboard, it is clear that there are different colors for each metric. Each of the factors that we decided to be of the greatest importance are represented with different colors. For instance Economy (GDP per capita) is green, while Freedom is grey, etc. We chose to do this so that when the slicer is used, it is easier for the user to distinguish which factor they are looking at. We also decided to include multiple features that make the dashboard more interactive. The average indicator is useful to show the averages when the user uses any of the filters. We chose to have the average indicator to be part of the slicer rather than just show the world average because it shows where each of the observations within the slicer fall in comparison to one another. The last notable feature we decided to include was the ability to select a section of the choropleth, rather than relying on one of the available filters. This allows the user to select countries that are not are grouped by one of the filters.
